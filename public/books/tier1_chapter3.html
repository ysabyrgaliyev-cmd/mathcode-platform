Section 1 of 5

🎵Patterns are everywhere.

Hi again! 🐵 Look at this row. What comes next?

🍌 🌿 🍌 🌿 🍌 🌿 ___

Easy — a 🍌, right? That's a pattern: something that repeats in order. Patterns are everywhere — in music (clap-stomp-clap-stomp), in tigers (stripe-skin-stripe-skin), in numbers (2, 4, 6, 8...).

A pattern in numbers

2, 4, 6, 8, 10... see what's happening? We add 2 every time. The jump stays the same. That's a number pattern.

Counting one-by-one (1, 2, 3, 4...) is fine. But sometimes we want to jump — count by 2s, by 5s, by 10s. That's faster and it shows a pattern. Coders love that.

This whole chapter, we'll teach the computer to count in jumps, then we'll teach a little turtle 🐢 to draw shapes using a loop. Turn the page!

Section 2 of 5

2️⃣Counting by 2s.

Remember the loop from Chapter 1?

for i in range(5):
    print(i)

That printed 0, 1, 2, 3, 4 — counting by 1s, starting at 0. Today we'll teach range a new trick. It can take three numbers:

for i in range(0, 10, 2):
    print(i)

Read the three numbers as: "start at 0, stop before 10, jump by 2." So Python prints: 0, 2, 4, 6, 8. Five numbers, counting by 2s. ✨

range(0, 10, 2)Start at 0, stop before 10, jump by 2.
startThe first number you say.
stopThe number you don't reach. (Sneaky! It's "before this.")
stepHow big each jump is.

Section 3 of 5

🖐️Counting by 5s and 10s.

The same trick works for any jump size. Want to count by 5s? Just say 5:

for i in range(5, 50, 5):
    print(i)

That prints 5, 10, 15, 20, 25, 30, 35, 40, 45. The start is 5 this time (not 0), so we don't print 0. The jump is 5.

Real-world check

Count nickels: 5, 10, 15, 20 cents. That's exactly range(5, 25, 5)! Code matches real life.

You'll get to count by 10s up to 100 in the practice lab — that's nine jumps in a single line of code. Next, a sneaky way the same trick gives you multiplication. Turn the page. →

Section 4 of 5

✖️Sneaky multiplication.

Here's a secret older kids learn called the times table. Don't run away — you already know most of it!

Counting by 5s gives: 5, 10, 15, 20, 25... Those are 5×1, 5×2, 5×3, 5×4, 5×5. The "5 times table"! Loops can build the whole table for you:

for i in range(1, 6):
    print("5 ×", i, "=", 5 * i)

The little * (star) means "multiply" in code. So 5 * i means "5 times i."

*Multiply (like the × in math class).

You'll build the 3 times table in the practice lab — five lines of 3, 6, 9, 12, 15 from a single loop. One last surprise: a turtle who draws shapes. Turn the page. →

Section 5 of 5

🐢The turtle draws a square.

Meet Tully the Turtle. Tully holds a pen. When you say forward, she walks forward and the pen draws a line. When you say turn, she spins.

To draw a square, what does Tully need to do? Walk forward, turn, walk forward, turn — four times. A square has 4 sides. So...

for i in range(4):
    forward(60)
    turn(90)

Read it: "4 times: walk 60 steps forward, then turn 90°." A square has four 90° corners — that's why turn(90).

Magic fact

For any shape with N equal sides, the turn is 360 / N degrees. A triangle (3 sides) → 120°. A square (4) → 90°. A hexagon (6) → 60°.
